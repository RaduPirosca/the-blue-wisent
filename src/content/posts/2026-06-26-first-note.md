Board gaming feels like a natural evolution of the video gaming interest as one grows older. The latter uses a computer capable of _billions_ of operations every second to calculate the rules, what happens after your next action and ensure nothing is broken (though there are still bugs and glitches, which leads to some [fun speed runs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mSFHKAvTGNk)). On the board you need to rely on the humans playing to do all those calculations, and they are not only slower, but you need to make sure they are [not getting bored or overwhelmed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gUrRsx-F_bs).

It is precisely these limitations, coupled with the satisfying tactility that comes from high quality components, that I think make board games such an attractive medium. You get really unique combinations of **visual themes, story-telling through gameplay, as well as clever puzzles** that stem from combinations of easy-to-understand rules. It's no wonder we were instantly hooked when we bought our first big modern board games a few years back.[^first-board-games]

[^first-board-games]: The first modern board game I tried is [Scythe](https://stonemaiergames.com/games/scythe/), when one of my good friends brought it over to play in our first year of college. It was fun, but felt **too complicated** for me. After more years passed, college was over and a pandemic was over us, my fiance and I wanted to try something new so we bought a game we saw at our local hobby store: [Dice Throne](https://shop.dicethrone.com) (_two characters from Season One_). We were instantly hooked, so we went and bought all the Season Two characters, and soon after tried some different board games, with [Now or Never](https://www.redravengames.com/now-or-never/) and [Vivid Memories](https://floodgate.games/products/vivid?srsltid=AfmBOoqjtabmH99P7dDUrXwtPFcAu-KMk8WtZt-IVucMn3aeMLZEfp2p). This was the start of a great new hobby.
