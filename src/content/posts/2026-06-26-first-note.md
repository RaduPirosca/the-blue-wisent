Board gaming feels like a natural evolution of the video gaming interest as one grows older. The latter uses a computer capable of _billions_ of operations every second to calculate the rules, what happens after your next action and ensure nothing is broken (though there are still bugs and glitches, which leads to some [fun speed runs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mSFHKAvTGNk)). On the board you need to rely on the humans playing to do all those calculations, and they are not only slower, but you need to make sure they are [not getting bored or overwhelmed](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gUrRsx-F_bs).

It is precisely these limitations, coupled with the satisfying tactility that comes from high quality components, that I think make board games such an attractive medium. You get really unique combinations of **visual themes, story-telling through gameplay, as well as clever puzzles** that stem from combinations of easy-to-understand rules. It's no wonder we were instantly hooked when we bought our first big modern board games a few years back.[^first-board-games]


## The Start of our UKGE Journey

![The huge space of one of the halls in UKGE 2025](/images/posts/ukge2026/ukge2025-big-hall.jpeg "One of the halls in UKGE 2025")

Tickets to the UK Games Expo were a Christmas present from my fiance, seeing as our _board game_ hobby grew very fast in the years before.[^christmas-present-tickets] We were gonna go for the full weekend, flying from Bucharest to Birmingham, and doing the trip just for this expo.[^convenient-location]

The first feeling I had stepping through the gates was grandeur. It was our first con outside of Romania, and the difference in scale is immediately visible (over [42,000 people](https://boardgamewire.com/index.php/2025/06/03/uk-games-expo-now-fully-on-the-world-stage-of-mega-cons-as-record-breaking-event-surpasses-gen-con-for-trade-hall-size/) attended). Spanning three different halls and **almost 800 exhibitors**, you quickly felt like you won't have time over two days to experience even half of it. But going from stall to stall, we saw that you could take it step by step. I would split the activities you can do in the following categories:
- Visit an exhibitor's stall: see their products, creations and talk to them
- Quick demos of new/existing/upcoming games
- Full on playthroughs of a board game
- Playtest board game prototypes
- Attend talks and events of game designers/industry people/content creators
- Play the games you bought at the Open Play Area
- (_this was a surprise_) Visit the Living History Viking/Anglo-Saxon village put up by [historical reenactors](https://www.ardenweard.org/pages/2016/06/28/living-history/)

It's important to note that UKGE is not a board game convention, it's a **hobby game convention**. That means that you can find anything from [board games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Board_game) to [card games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Card_game), [role-playing games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Role-playing_game), [miniature wargames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miniature_wargame) and even [megagames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megagame). With such a big variety it's a good change to get outside your comfort zone and try out some new stuff, as we bought some GM-less RPG books to try our hand out at role-playing.

[^christmas-present-tickets]: Over the past three years Christmas has become an opportunity for her to organize great trips and experiences together. It's a great pretext to go outside our comfort zone, and so far we've done a concert in Belgrade, the UKGE and a snowboarding trip.

[^convenient-location]: UKGE is very convenient to go to. It happens at the [National Exhibition Center](https://www.thenec.co.uk), which is a huge convention center right next to the Birmingham airport. Combined with the fact that there are numerous hotels right next to the airport too, it is very easy to do the whole trip just by walking from one place to the other (or using the [Air-Rail link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air-Rail_Link)), without having to waste time commuting. But it does mean we haven't visited the city of Birmingham yet.
